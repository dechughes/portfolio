## **Why I Built This**

This project sits somewhere before all of that, in how apps like TikTok, Instagram or Candy Crush (CC still has 170+ million monthly users - who is playing this?) have used techniques to keep people on their platforms. Before we gave all of our data away to the AI overlords, this is how companies learned about us.

I wanted to see if I could design and code a small experience that uses the basic behavioural techniques apps rely on to keep people engaged. I wanted to understand what these patterns look like when you build them from scratch, how they feel in practice and what the user experiences on the other side.

Because I designed it, I can’t be a test subject. So this version is simply the prototype. V2 will be about getting real people to use it, collecting data and seeing how different behaviours emerge.

The aim of V1 was simple: get the idea out of my head and into a working prototype. Companies like King (Candy Crush) and TikTok have been doing this kind of behavioural shaping for years, but their data is locked away. I wanted to build something small that exposes the same mechanics in a transparent way.

## **Design Intent**

When I started, I wanted the experience to feel controlled, slightly ambiguous and not gimmicky. Almost like the loading bars on phones where the percentage is intentionally misleading. I’ve always wondered whether 15–20 percent battery has been psychologically reframed for us. When my phone hits 20 percent, it feels like it’s minutes from dying. Is that engineered? Definitely.

---

## **Behavioural Mechanics Built Into the Loop**

- **Artificial progress:** Cycle 1 jumps to 70 percent to create early momentum.  
- **Variable reward:** A small glyph appears only when a cycle completes.  
- **Goal‑gradient tension:** Each cycle moves the bar a little less.  
- **False endings:** Cycle 5 looks like the end but isn’t.  
- **Controlled friction:** Button delays increase each cycle.  
- **Adaptive delay:** Fast taps increase friction, slow taps reduce it.  
- **Micro‑reward animation:** A small floating glyph that feels earned.  
- **Loss aversion:** After cycle 2, a message appears: leaving resets progress.  
- **Commitment cue:** The entry CTA shrinks when tapped.  
- **Visual urgency:** Subtle pulses and timing shifts.  
- **False sense of control:** You feel like you’re choosing the pace.  
- **Reveal transition:** The loop collapses and the reveal fades in.

The cycle copy was intentionally minimal and slightly unusual. It needed to feel intentional without being instructional. Soft authority in a sense.

## **How I Built This**

I didn’t build this alone. I used AI as a design partner rather than a code generator. We iterated on the loop architecture, the timing, the copy, the transitions and the reveal. Every part of the experience was shaped through back‑and‑forth refinement. I would sketch the intention, the AI would propose a structure, I would push it further, and we would tighten it together. The result feels designed, not autogenerated. The AI helped me think through the psychology, the pacing and the micro‑behaviours in a way that sped up the process without removing my voice from the project.

## **What I Would Do in V2**

From V2 onwards I would like to do A/B testing on the different screens and interactions that happen to the user. It would be interesting to see what behaviours encourage usage and whether there is a way to make this all more implicit so users don’t even notice the behaviours they’re being nudged into.

## **Conclusion**

Overall it’s scary how quickly companies have optimised for this type of behaviour, whereby key roles in these companies aren’t always held by the engineers but by the behavioural psychologists and attention designers who ensure we stay locked on our screens.

What has the experiment taught me? That a lot of thinking goes into every second of your attention, and that each small pull draws you deeper into a haze of not quitting. Ten or fifteen seconds doesn’t sound like much, but over a lifetime this can account for hours of quiet influence and subtle manipulation. Just a thought.
