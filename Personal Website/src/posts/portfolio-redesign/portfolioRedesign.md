## Context

My original portfolio (V1) was built in December 2025. At the time, I wanted a simple place to collect the projects and ideas I was working on outside of my day job. Before that, everything lived in scattered notebooks and half-finished notes, none of which reflected the skills I was actually building.

Building it also gave me a reason to learn HTML, CSS, React, and the basics of front-end structure. AI tools helped me move faster, but understanding how things fit together was genuinely enjoyable — and surprisingly motivating.

Having a portfolio also keeps me accountable. If I'm going to keep experimenting, learning, and documenting what I've built — and where I've gone wrong — I need a place to put it.

![Before – V1 Homepage](../assets/portfolio-v2/before-home.png "V1 — December 2025")

The redesign came after finishing the Deliveroo teardown. I realised the level of effort I was putting into projects wasn't being matched by the design of the site hosting them. V1 did its job, but it wasn't doing the work justice.

The original design was intentionally minimal — echoing portfolios like Laurel Schwulst's and Eric Meyer's, where the main principle is to exist and be functional. It was never meant to be the final version, just a foundation to start publishing from.

![Before – V1 Project Page](../assets/portfolio-v2/before-project.png "V1 project layout")

---

## Problem

The core issue with V1 was aesthetic. It hit what it set out to do — functional without the flourish — but looking back at it now, it reads as close to brutalist. Simplified to the point of feeling unfinished.

It lacked personality, rhythm, and any kind of cohesive design system. And if I want to learn more about design — typography, layout, motifs, editorial structure — my own site is the best place to practice.

V2 was the natural next step.

---

## Insight

V2 needed to feel more editorial — not in a decorative sense, but in how the content flows and how the page communicates before anyone reads a word.

Good design changes how something is perceived, even when the underlying content stays the same. The same way your clothes shape how people read you before you speak, the design of a portfolio shapes how people read your work before they engage with it.

V1 was honest. It just didn't reflect the level of care behind the projects.

![V2 Homepage](../assets/portfolio-v2/after-home.png "V2 — April 2026")

---

## Strategy

The strategy was to improve without rebuilding. The posts reflect where my skills were at the time they were written — that's part of the point. If I'd known Power BI earlier, the SQL project would have looked different. The writing and thinking stay as they were.

What changed was the presentation layer:

- Typography and spacing tightened throughout
- A motif system introduced for visual identity — a strip motif and floral wash creating depth without noise
- Hover states and subtle colour cues added across interactive elements
- The long alternating project layout replaced with a compact editorial breakdown
- An image carousel added to give visuals more room and reduce scrolling
- Journal entries moved higher so project pages feel more alive
- All layout changes scoped to the Projects page so the rest of the site remained stable

![Motif Detail](../assets/portfolio-v2/motif-detail.png "The strip motif — fixed to the left viewport edge")


This wasn't a full rebuild. It was a controlled redesign within realistic constraints — knowing what I could build, what I could explain, and what I could maintain.

---

## Outcome

A cleaner, more intentional portfolio. The projects read better. The visuals have space. The site has a bit of personality now — somewhere between editorial and functional — without losing the simplicity that made V1 work.

![V2 Project Layout](../assets/portfolio-v2/after-project.png "V2 project detail — carousel + compact breakdown")

---

## Reflection

Redesigning is a compromise. You have to balance what you want with what's realistic, what you can build, and what you can maintain.

I didn't want to spend 20+ hours rebuilding everything from scratch, and I didn't want to introduce complexity I couldn't explain or justify. The decisions that feel most considered in V2 are the ones where I found a simpler solution than the one I originally reached for.

That's probably true of most design work.
