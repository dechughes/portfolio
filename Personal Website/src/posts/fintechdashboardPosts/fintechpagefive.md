## **Findings**

### **Growth**

New user sign-ups are consistent throughout 2024 illustrated by steady acquisition with natural month-to-month fluctuation but no meaningful acceleration or decline. On the surface, this looks healthy. But revenue tells a completely different story: total revenue falls continuously from January to December, dropping from roughly $308 average revenue per active subscriber down to $23 by year end.


The reason for this apparent contradiction-steady sign‑ups but falling revenue-is the revenue model. Users only generate revenue after converting to paid. Once converted, they generate subscription fees and transaction fees every billing month but both stop the moment they churn. So revenue at any given point in time is almost entirely determined by how many users from previous months are still subscribed. That pool shrinks every month as churn takes effect, and late-year sign-ups haven't had enough time converting and billing to compensate. Instead of having a steady user base, the business is cycling through monthly user cohorts rather than compounding them.

---

### **Monetisation**

The funnel metrics are strong:

- **59.9%** of sign‑ups activate  
- **41.5%** of activated users convert to paid  

This is far above the fintech industry norm of **5–14%** for moving from sign‑up to a key monetisation action.

Revenue is also well‑distributed across acquisition channels:

- Organic: **$1.52M**  
- Paid Social: **$1.13M**  
- Referral: **$767k**  
- Influencer: **$377k**  

Each channel contributes proportionally to its user volume, indicating no channel is disproportionately over‑ or under‑performing. The acquisition engine is healthy and diversified.

However, only **24.83%** of all sign‑ups ever generate revenue. The largest points of attrition are:

- **40%** of sign‑ups never activate  
- **58.5%** of activated users never convert  

This is expected in a freemium model, but it means the business is heavily reliant on the quality of its conversion mechanics and ARPU to sustain growth - there is limited room to absorb further user drop-off.

---

### **Retention and Churn**

The churn story is more complex due to limitations in the dataset.

Monthly churn appears to fall dramatically over the year-from **26% in January** to **5% in December**. This looks like improving retention, but it is not.

- January’s active subscriber base is dominated by early converts who have already self‑selected; low‑intent users churned earlier, leaving only committed subscribers.  
- December’s active base includes both long‑surviving early users and very recent converts who have not yet had time to churn.  

Both effects artificially suppress churn.

The most reliable period is **May-July**, where distortions are minimal. In this window, monthly churn stabilises around **15-18%**. This is higher than the model’s assumed **4%**, but the gap is methodological: churn is being calculated against the remaining active subscriber pool rather than the start‑of‑month base. As the active pool shrinks, this inflates the apparent churn rate.

What is clear is that the active subscriber base declines from **~2,043 in January** to **~652 in December**. Revenue follows this decline directly. The business is not compounding subscriber value; it is replacing churned users with new ones, but not fast enough to grow the base net of churn.

---

## **Summary**

The business has strong diversified user acquisition and above-average conversion for fintech. The challenge sits further down the lifecycle. Revenue declines across the year because early cohorts are churning faster than later cohorts can replace them, meaning the subscriber base is shrinking even as new users continue to arrive. The funnel is functioning; the leakage is happening after the sale.

The commercial implication is straightforward: improving retention in the first one to two months post‑conversion would have a far greater impact on revenue than any incremental gains at the top of the funnel. Until early‑life churn is reduced, the business will continue replacing lost subscribers rather than compounding value from existing ones.

From a CEO’s perspective, this is a company with a high‑performing acquisition engine and a retention problem that remains unsolved. The path to durable, compounding revenue runs through strengthening early‑stage retention, not driving more traffic into the funnel.
