## **Key Questions**

- Does one experiment group activate better?  
- Does one convert better?  
- Does one produce higher ARPU or revenue per user?  
- Does one retain better or churn less?  
- Are differences statistically meaningful or commercially negligible?  
- Would rolling out the winning variant materially impact revenue?  
- Should leadership roll out A, B, or run further tests?

## **Findings**

### **Performance Comparison**

#### **Activation**
Activation rates for A and B differ by 0.01%, indicating no meaningful behavioural difference at the top of the funnel.

#### **Conversion**
Conversion from activation to paid user differs by 0.2%, with Variant B slightly outperforming Variant A. This difference is extremely small and commercially negligible.

#### **ARPU and Revenue per User**
ARPU and revenue per user are effectively identical across both variants. Neither variant produces higher monetisation.

#### **Churn and Retention**
Churn rate across the experiment is 4.26%, with:  
- Churn Rate A: 4.22%  
- Churn Rate B: 4.29%  

This difference (0.07 percentage points) is statistically insignificant and commercially irrelevant. Neither variant introduces retention risk or benefit.

---

## **Statistical vs Commercial Impact**

### **Statistical Significance**
All differences between A and B fall within noise‑level variation. There is no evidence that either variant meaningfully changes user behaviour.

### **Commercial Significance**
Because activation, conversion, ARPU, revenue per user, and churn are all effectively identical, rolling out Variant B would produce no measurable revenue uplift. Even at scale, the impact would be negligible.

### **Implementation Cost**
As there is no cost associated with rolling out either variant, even with zero cost, the commercial benefit is too small to justify prioritisation.

---

## **Recommendation**

Variant B performs marginally better in conversion and marginally worse in churn, but the differences are so small that they are commercially meaningless. There is no compelling reason to roll out either variant. Due to this, the recommendation is further testing with more substantial UX or product changes.

This reflects a common real-world scenario where experiments fail to produce signal - highlighting the importance of testing meaningful product changes rather than incremental UI tweaks.
